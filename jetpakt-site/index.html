The five operating pillars

A restaurant is five systems. Drift shows up in one, then spreads.

The Restaurant Operating System framework treats operating a restaurant as an engineering problem with a human core. Every JetPakt diagnosis maps public evidence to one of the five pillars, to a specific drift case, and to the operating principles that resolve it.

01 · Production

Kitchen

Every plate built to spec, every shift. Drift shows up as food-quality variance, expo bottlenecks, and safety-log skips. Mapped principles: Six Sigma, Theory of Constraints, Reliability Engineering.

Cases I01 through I05

02 · Service

Front of house

Hospitality engineered as a system. Drift shows up as pacing complaints, inconsistent steps of service, and ambience degradation. Mapped principles: Drucker, Situational Leadership, Broken Windows.

Cases I06 through I09

03 · Sales

Menu and demand

The menu as an engine, not a list. Drift shows up as pricing-value complaints, check-size compression, and fee-transparency issues. Mapped principles: Menu Engineering, Kahneman, Game Theory.

Cases I10 through I13

04 · Operations

Infrastructure

The restaurant as a reliable machine. Drift shows up as staffing fragility, peak-hour collapse, and turnover-driven understaffing. Mapped principles: Herzberg, Systems Thinking.

Cases I14 through I18

05 · Management

Leadership rhythm

The weekly cadence that compounds excellence. Drift shows up as billing-control issues, P&L blind spots, and cash-handling exceptions. Mapped principles: Principal-Agent, Verification, Weekly Review.

Cases I19 through I24

Why this, why now

Reviews are a symptom. The drift showed up in the dining room first.

Independent operators are navigating tighter margins, a tougher labor market, and a new wave of fee-disclosure legislation. A rating slide rarely announces itself on one bad night, it accumulates across shifts that quietly drift, a pre-shift that gets skipped, a portion that creeps, a check-back that disappears. By the time the reviews catch up, covers have already moved.

JetPakt is the diagnostic layer that catches drift in the public record before it compounds. We read the signals, name the pillar, match the case, compute the recovery, and hand the owner a plan. Software finds the pattern. The owner makes the call.

What the engine actually does

The method is boring. The output is not.

Most reputation tools sell automation, auto-requests, auto-responses, mass generation. JetPakt runs in the opposite direction. Six disciplines, applied the same way for every owner, in every state.

01

Ten hospitality-specific signals

Food quality, food safety, service pacing, service-fee transparency, cleanliness, staffing, billing disputes, noise ambience, server attitude, pricing value. Generic sentiment treats a billing-disputes 1-star the same as a noise-ambience 1-star. We do not.

02

Severity with recency half-life

Review signals decay over time; operational drift signals persist because the underlying condition usually does. An old, unaddressed pacing complaint still counts. Math, not optics.

03

Peer benchmark with shrinkage

Bayesian shrinkage against your local peer bucket so a tiny-count outlier does not dominate the average. Your peer delta is honest, not cherry-picked.

04

Pillar rollup and case match

Every signal carries a pillar tag and Restaurant Operating System case references (I01 through I24). The memo's headline is which pillar owns the drift, not which review stung the most.

05

Revenue-recovery range, cited

Luca (HBS) rating elasticity, Anderson-Magruder peak-hour uplift, One-Haus local check averages, Denver CVB labor load. Range, not point estimate. Every input and URL printed in the memo footnote.

06

Legal-flag routing, drafts only

Wage, fee, disclosure, and food-safety language is surfaced and routed for your attorney before any response is drafted. Every response lands as a draft. Nothing is auto-posted. Nothing names individual staff.

Frequently asked

Questions we get from owners.

Do you post responses on our behalf?

No. Everything we produce is a draft sent to the owner's inbox. You review, edit if needed, and post. We never auto-post to Google, Yelp, or any public channel.

Will you name our employees?

No. Our audits and drafts never name individual staff, even when reviewers do. Staff first names only appear inside a verbatim quoted review where we need to preserve the source, and even then we will flag it for your attention.

Is this review-response software?

No. Review response is one output of one layer of the product. The core product is a drift diagnosis across five operating pillars, Production, Service, Sales, Operations, Management, with a revenue-recovery range and a 30 / 60 / 90 owner plan. Reviews are the cheapest public signal your business leaks, so they are the front door, but they are not the product.

What does the operator memo actually look like?

One page, operator-legible. Top block: dominant-pillar rollup, Restaurant Operating System case match, monthly revenue-recovery range. Middle block: evidence, verbatim, with source URLs. Bottom block: 30 / 60 / 90 plan and legal-flag list. Request a free preview and we will send a one-page sample drawn entirely from your public record within two business days.

How is the revenue-recovery number calculated?

Luca (HBS 12-016) rating elasticity, Anderson-Magruder peak-hour uplift, One-Haus local check averages, Denver CVB labor-load figures. Range, not point estimate. Every input and URL is printed in the memo footnote. No dashboards, no black box.

How is this different from a reputation management service?

Most reputation tools sell automation: auto-requests, auto-responses, mass review generation. JetPakt is a drift-diagnosis system that uses reviews as one of several inputs. You get a diagnosis with a dollar figure and a plan, not a dashboard and a monthly auto-posting quota.

Do you work with chains or franchises?

At this stage we focus on independent, owner-operated restaurants with fewer than five locations. Chains and franchise units are outside our current scope, since they typically have corporate reputation teams with different priorities.

Do you work nationwide?

Yes. The method is portable because the inputs are universal: public review text, public records, and the owner's own context. Jurisdiction-specific statutes are tracked when they apply to your business, including Colorado HB25 1090 for Colorado operators and comparable fee-disclosure and wage statutes elsewhere.

What does HB25 1090 have to do with my business?

Colorado's HB25 1090 (Junk Fees Law) took effect January 1, 2026. It changes how service fees and automatic charges must be disclosed for businesses operating in Colorado. If you operate in Colorado, we track every mention of service-fee, surcharge, or automatic-gratuity language in your public reviews and flag items that could attract regulatory attention or a private-action suit. Similar statutes in other states are flagged the same way. Your attorney makes the legal call. We just make sure nothing is missed.

Can I cancel a Pulse subscription?

Anytime. No contracts, no cancellation fees. Cancel and we stop the next cycle. You keep every PDF and draft already delivered.
